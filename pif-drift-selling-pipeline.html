What's Happening Here

On April 1, 2026, the Drift Protocol was exploited for $285 million. The attacker drained the protocol's vaults and moved the stolen funds across multiple blockchains. That story is well-documented.

But there's a second story playing out at the same time: someone is aggressively selling the DRIFT token itself. DRIFT is the governance token of the protocol — it's what holders use to vote on changes and what traders buy when they believe in the project's future. When millions of tokens get dumped on exchanges right after an exploit, it accelerates the price crash and compounds the damage to everyone still holding.

We traced two parallel selling pipelines. One belongs to the attacker. The other belongs to someone nobody can identify — and the Drift team says it's not them.

What is a governance token? A governance token like DRIFT gives holders voting power over a protocol's decisions — things like fee structures, new features, or treasury spending. It also trades on exchanges like any other cryptocurrency. When its price crashes, it signals lost confidence in the project and reduces the protocol's ability to raise funds or attract users.

The Correction: It's Not the Team

Important update — April 4, 2026
On April 3, wallet G1kTzz...S1FaX was flagged by OnchainLens and labeled by Arkham Intelligence as the "Drift Protocol Team" wallet. This label was picked up across crypto Twitter and appeared in our initial report. An independent security firm later reported that Drift denied ownership of this wallet — however, Drift has not posted any official public statement confirming or denying this. Until they do, the attribution remains unverified. The Arkham label is unconfirmed.
What is Arkham Intelligence? Arkham is a blockchain analytics platform that labels wallet addresses — identifying which wallets belong to which companies, traders, or entities. These labels are partly algorithmic and partly community-submitted. They are not verified ownership records. When Arkham labels a wallet "Drift Protocol Team," it's an educated guess based on patterns, not a confirmation from Drift. Whether this label is accurate has not been officially addressed by the Drift team.

This means the wallet dumping 56 million DRIFT tokens across Bybit and Gate is unidentified. The DRIFT token account dates to October 2024. The transactions use durable nonces. The holder remains unknown.

What are durable nonces? Normally on Solana, a transaction must be submitted within about 90 seconds or it expires. Durable nonces bypass this — they let you pre-sign a transaction and submit it hours, days, or weeks later. The Drift attacker used this technique to prepare the exploit in advance. The fact that the DRIFT token seller is also using durable nonces is a red flag — it's the same uncommon technique, being used by a different unknown wallet, during the same time window.

The Selling Pipeline — Traced On-Chain

We traced the full path of the DRIFT tokens, from their original source to the exchange deposit addresses where they were sold. Here's the chain:

Pipeline 1: The CEX dump chain — Bybit and Gate deposits · independently verified
What is a CEX? A centralized exchange (CEX) like Bybit, Gate, or Binance is a platform where you can trade cryptocurrency for cash or other tokens. When someone deposits tokens to a CEX, they're typically converting them to cash. Depositing 56 million DRIFT tokens to Bybit and Gate means someone is selling — converting DRIFT into money they can withdraw.
Why does the origin matter? These DRIFT tokens trace back to a wallet that distributed 95.67 million tokens in May 2024 — the month DRIFT launched. That's a vesting or treasury wallet. This means the tokens being sold now were allocated at launch, sat dormant for 18 months, and were activated 5 hours after the protocol got exploited for $285 million. The timing raises the question: does the seller know something, or are they just panicking?

Pipeline 2: The Attacker's Bot Is Also Selling DRIFT

Separate from the CEX pipeline above, the attacker's own dispersion bot — MfDuWe...GVWa — is independently selling DRIFT tokens on Solana DEXes.

What is a DEX? A decentralized exchange (DEX) like Jupiter or Raydium lets you swap tokens directly on the blockchain without an account, ID, or intermediary. Unlike a CEX, there's no company in the middle. The attacker is using DEXes because they require no identity verification — you just connect a wallet and swap.

Pipeline 3: The Distribution Hub

Beyond the two pipelines above, a third network of wallets has been selling DRIFT through Solana DEXes. These wallets are all fed by a single distribution hub: BCJtr7...MypH.

What is a distribution hub? In on-chain forensics, a "hub" is a wallet that receives tokens from a few sources and sends them to many destinations. Think of it like a warehouse — tokens come in through one door and go out through fifteen. This pattern is used to spread selling across many wallets so it doesn't look like one entity dumping. Each downstream wallet sells a smaller amount, making the selling pressure harder to detect.

Why does selling speed matter? Tokens are like seats on a lifeboat. The first seller gets the best price because there are still buyers willing to pay near market value. As more sellers pile in, the price drops. By the time the attacker's bot started dumping DRIFT, the price had already fallen 30%. The early sellers got out near the top. The late sellers — including the attacker — sold into a crashed market.

Summary — What Happened and Why It Matters

The Drift Protocol was exploited for $285 million on April 1, 2026. While the immediate theft has been widely covered, a parallel story went largely unnoticed: someone started aggressively selling the DRIFT governance token within hours of the exploit.

Three separate selling pipelines are operating simultaneously:

  • Pipeline 1 — The CEX Dumps: An unidentified wallet moved 58.18 million DRIFT to an intermediary, which deposited 56.25 million tokens to Bybit and Gate exchanges. The total value: approximately $2.4 million. These tokens trace back to a launch-era vesting wallet from May 2024. The intermediary used durable nonces — the same technique used in the exploit itself. Arkham Intelligence labeled the source as a "Drift Protocol Team" wallet. This has not been officially confirmed or denied by Drift. The owner remains unknown.
  • Pipeline 2 — The Attacker Bot: The same bot that dispersed $358 million in stolen USDC is also dumping 2.9 million DRIFT tokens through Solana DEXes. This means the attacker acquired DRIFT during the vault drain and is selling it through the same infrastructure used to launder the other stolen funds. Total USDC extracted: $102,489.
  • Pipeline 3 — The Distribution Hub: A wallet called BCJtr7 has been feeding DRIFT to 15+ downstream wallets that sell on DEXes. The top seller in this network, BMnT51, dumped 2.9 million DRIFT for $140,827 — starting just 86 minutes after the exploit when the price was still relatively high. This hub has been operating since January 2026, well before the exploit.

Why this matters:

  • The DRIFT token has lost 51% of its value since the exploit ($0.069 → $0.035) and is still falling. Every token dumped on the market pushes the price lower.
  • Token holders — the people who believed in Drift and deposited their money — are watching their investment evaporate while unknown wallets cash out millions.
  • The durable nonce connection between the CEX dumping pipeline and the exploit technique is unexplained. It could be coincidence. It could be coordination.
  • The Arkham labeling error is a reminder that on-chain labels are guesses, not facts. A wrong label can misdirect an entire investigation — in this case, initially pointing the finger at the Drift team for selling their own token during a crisis.

The bottom line: $285 million was stolen from the protocol. $2.4 million more is being extracted through token selling. The attacker is profiting from both the vault drain and the token crash. An unknown entity with launch-era tokens activated 5 hours after the exploit and dumped them using the same technique as the attacker. Nobody has explained who they are.
